Context

What Propel is.

Propel connects the African tech-talent ecosystem to the global workforce. It runs as a three-sided network.

On one side, hundreds of thousands of members across specialised tech communities. On another, community leaders who organise and grow them. On the third, companies who tap that network on-demand to hire, beta-test, and run hackathons. The product's job is to make value flow between all three, and to keep talent equipped enough to actually show up for the opportunity, which is where financing comes in.

Talent Community leaders Companies Embedded finance

The problem

Everyone was one tab away from giving up.

The opportunity was real, but the experience asked too much of people who already had the least margin for friction.

Talent bounced early

Long, generic onboarding asked for everything up front and matched poorly. People left before they ever saw a relevant role.

Communities had no reason to stay

Leaders couldn't see member outcomes or earn from them. Without proof of value, engagement and revenue both stalled.

Companies couldn't trust the pipeline

Recruiters got volume, not signal, no shared view of vetting, community context, or relocation-readiness.

A platform that connects three sides only works if all three feel the value on day one.

Discovery & research

I went looking for where trust breaks.

A mixed-methods sprint to understand all three sides before drawing a single screen.

Method

24 interviews

13 talent, 6 community leaders, 5 recruiters across 4 countries.

Method

610 survey responses

Members ranking their biggest blockers to getting hired.

Method

Funnel teardown

Drop-off analysis across signup → profile → apply.

Method

3 community ride-alongs

Shadowing leaders running events and job shares.

01

Relevance beats volume.

Members didn't want more jobs, they wanted to believe the few they saw were actually attainable. Match confidence, not catalogue size, drove whether they applied.

02

The blocker was often a device, not a skill.

A recurring, under-served story: qualified people stalling because of a broken laptop or upfront relocation costs. Capability was never the issue; cash-flow was.

03

Leaders are the trust layer.

Members trusted opportunities far more when they came through their community. But leaders had no tooling to surface, track, or get credit for those outcomes.

04

Companies pay for signal, not sourcing.

Recruiters valued community vetting and context over raw reach, but the product gave them a résumé pile with none of that context attached.

Framing the work

From insights to a point of view.

I reframed the brief from "add more features to three apps" into one question:

How might we make one account deliver three different jobs, without diluting any of them?

That led to four principles I held every decision against.

Principle 01

Show the match, not the maze

Lead with relevance and confidence. Earn the next tap before asking for more.

Principle 02

Remove the real blocker

If a device or upfront cost stands between talent and a job, treat that as a design problem.

Principle 03

Make leaders heroes

Give communities visible outcomes and real revenue. Their trust is the product.

Principle 04

Sell signal, not volume

Carry community vetting and context all the way to the recruiter.

Testing & iteration

What testing changed.

Two rounds of moderated usability tests across all three personas. The biggest wins came from getting out of people's way.

Before

  • •Profile asked for 14 fields before showing a single job.
  • •Match scores with no explanation read as arbitrary.
  • •Financing felt buried and slightly suspicious.
  • •Leaders couldn't tell which shares led to hires.

After

  • •Three taps to a first match; profile completes over time.
  • •Every score shows its reasoning and a way to improve it.
  • •Financing surfaced in-context with total cost up front.
  • •Leaders see hire attribution and payout side by side.

Reflection

What I'd carry forward.

→

Trust is an interface.

The highest-leverage design decisions weren't screens, they were where we chose to show vetting, attribution, and cost. Make trust visible and the funnel takes care of itself.

→

Design the blocker, not just the flow.

Embedding financing at the moment of need reframed what "product design" meant here: sometimes the kindest UX is removing a real-world barrier, not polishing a button.

→

Next: deepen the leader economy.

I'd explore richer payout models and community-to-community referrals, plus an offline-first mode for low-connectivity members.
